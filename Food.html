Great Indian Foods

Kafuli-( Uttarakhand )

Kafuli is a traditional and beloved green curry from the Garhwal region of Uttarakhand, distinct from the coastal Goan fish curry you also like. It is made primarily with leafy greens—mainly spinach (palak) and fenugreek leaves (methi)—and is known for its rich, earthy flavor and highly nutritious qualities. Kafuli is usually paired with steamed rice or local breads (mandua roti or phulka), making for a wholesome and comforting meal especially enjoyed in colder months.Kafuli offers a wonderful vegetarian addition to your culinary explorations—both nutritious and rooted deeply in Uttarakhand’s culture and lifestyle.



Kosha Mangsho-( West Bengal )

Kosha Mangsho is a beloved Bengali mutton (goat meat) curry, renowned for its deep, intensely rich flavors and thick, brown gravy. A specialty from West Bengal, this dish is a staple for festive occasions and Sunday lunches, often served with luchi (fried bread), paratha, or rice The term “kosha” refers to a style of slow-cooking, similar to ‘bhuna’, where meat is sautéed and cooked over low heat for a prolonged period. This technique results in tender meat and a luxuriously thick, caramelized gravy. The use of mustard oil, whole spices, onions, yogurt, and a touch of ghee gives the dish its aromatic, spicy, and slightly smoky character.



Dhokla-( Gujarat )

Dhokla is a traditional steamed, fermented snack native to the Indian state of Gujarat, with a history that stretches back nearly a thousand years. Its cultural importance in Gujarati cuisine is deeply rooted, making it both an everyday favorite and a symbol of Gujarati hospitality and celebration. Early references to a dish similar to dhokla, called dukkai, appear in Jain texts as early as 1068 AD, showing that the concept of steamed, fermented savory cakes is centuries old. The term "dhokla" itself appears in the Varanaka Samuchaya, a Gujarati text dating to around 1520 CE. Originally, dhokla was made by fermenting a mixed batter of rice and Bengal gram (chana dal) overnight and then steaming it. This age-old method highlights Gujarat’s inclination toward fermentation and steaming as healthy cooking techniques.



Rogan Josh-( Kashmir )

Rogan Josh is an iconic aromatic curried meat dish from Kashmir, and is one of the most celebrated dishes in Kashmiri cuisine as well as across the Indian subcontinent. It is particularly distinguished as a centerpiece in the grand Kashmiri multi-course meal called wazwan. The name "rogan josh" has Persian origins: “Rogan” can mean "clarified butter" or "oil," and “josh” refers to heat or boiling. Some interpretations also translate “rogan” as "red," referring to the dish's vibrant color. The dish was introduced to Kashmir by the Mughals, whose cuisine was heavily influenced by Persian culinary traditions



Pongal-( Tamil Nadu )

Pongal is a traditional and culturally significant dish originating from South India, especially from Tamil Nadu. The word "Pongal" is derived from the Tamil word pongu which means "to boil over" or "overflow," symbolizing abundance and prosperity linked to the harvest. Pongal has roots that go back centuries in South Indian culture, initially made as an offering to deities during harvest festivals to celebrate and give thanks for a bountiful crop. The dish is traditionally prepared by boiling rice and yellow moong dal (lentils) together until soft and creamy, often flavored with black pepper, cumin, ginger, curry leaves, and ghee. Sometimes cashew nuts are added for richness.



Papaya Khar-( Assam )

Papaya Khar is a traditional and popular Assamese dish made using raw (unripe) papaya and "khar," an alkaline ingredient derived from the ashes of sun-dried banana plant parts, mainly the skin of certain banana varieties. It is considered a digestive aid and is typically served as the first course in a traditional Assamese meal. Raw papaya, khar (alkaline extract from banana peel ashes), mustard oil, garlic, salt, and sometimes green chilies or spices like panch phoran (Assamese five-spice mix) are used. Moong dal (split yellow lentils) is sometimes added as well.



Litti Chokha-( Uttar Pradesh )

Litti Chokha is a traditional and popular dish from the Indian state of Bihar, also common in eastern Uttar Pradesh and parts of Nepal. It consists of two main components: Litti: Whole wheat dough balls stuffed with a spiced mixture of sattu (roasted gram/chickpea flour) blended with herbs and spices like cumin, fennel, carom seeds, nigella, garlic, ginger, green chilies, coriander, salt, lemon juice, and mustard oil. These dough balls are typically roasted over charcoal or cow dung fire, giving them a distinctive smoky flavor, though they can also be baked or pan-fried. Chokha: A mashed vegetable preparation, traditionally made by roasting or boiling eggplant (baingan), tomatoes, and potatoes, then mashing them with ingredients like chopped onions, garlic, green chilies, mustard oil, salt, coriander leaves, and sometimes lemon juice. Variations include Baingan Chokha (eggplant), Tomato Chokha, and Aloo Chokha (potato).



Makki di Roti-( Punjab )

Makki di Roti (cornmeal flatbread) and Sarson da Saag (mustard greens curry) are classic dishes from Punjabi cuisine, especially enjoyed during North India's winter months. They are celebrated for their rustic flavors, nutritional value, and their deep connection with Punjab's agrarian culture. Makki di Roti is an unleavened flatbread made from maize (corn) flour. It has a slightly sweet, earthy taste and is typically thicker than wheat flour rotis. The bread is often enjoyed hot, slathered with white butter or ghee, and served with jaggery (gud) on the side for an authentic Punjabi experience.



Dhaam-( Himachal Pradesh )

Dhaam in Himachal Pradesh is a traditional festive meal served during special occasions such as marriages, birthdays, retirements, and religious events. It is a multi-course lunch prepared and served on leaf plates called pattal to people sitting on the floor, reflecting the rich culinary culture of the state. The food is cooked almost exclusively by a special Brahmin community called botis, who prepare it in large brass vessels called baltohi or charoti and only use traditional methods like slow cooking on wood fire. The typical Himachali Dhaam usually starts with plain aromatic rice, followed by madra (a yogurt-based chickpea or rajma curry cooked with about twenty spices), various dals like mash dal, spicy vegetable curries, khatta (a sweet and sour tamarind-jaggery sauce), paneer, and ends with meetha bhaat — a sweet rice dessert.



Pootharekulu-( Andhra Pradesh )

Pootharekulu (also spelled Putharekulu) is a traditional Indian sweet from Atreyapuram, a village in the East Godavari district of Andhra Pradesh, famous in Telugu-speaking regions. The name means "coated sheets" in Telugu, referring to the wafer-thin rice starch sheets that wrap the sweet filling. The sweet consists of ultra-thin rice paper layers made from a special rice batter, which are carefully spread on a heated, inverted earthen pot, forming delicate, paper-like sheets. These sheets are then stuffed with a mixture of powdered sugar or jaggery and ghee (clarified butter), often with dry fruits and nuts, before being expertly folded into rolls.



Hyderabadi Biryani-( Telangana )

Hyderabadi Biryani became refined under the Nizams, who employed master chefs (khansamas) to craft this regal dish using the "dum pukht" technique—slow cooking raw marinated meat (usually goat or chicken) layered with half-cooked basmati rice sealed in a pot with dough and cooked over low heat. This method blends aromatic spices, fried onions, mint, yogurt, and saffron into the meat and rice, delivering a rich, fragrant, and tender culinary experience. From its royal palace origins, Hyderabadi Biryani evolved into a beloved street and home food staple, spreading its fame across India and worldwide, now being celebrated in food festivals and restaurants globally.



Fish curry-( Goa )

Fish curry is a diverse dish enjoyed in many regional variations, typically featuring fish cooked in a spiced, often tangy and sometimes creamy sauce. It commonly includes a blend of ingredients like onions, garlic, ginger, tomatoes, curry leaves, and various spices such as turmeric, chili powder, mustard seeds, fenugreek, and garam masala. Coconut milk or grated coconut is frequently used to add richness, particularly in South Indian and Goan styles. A basic approach to making fish curry usually involves sautéing aromatic spices and ingredients, then simmering the fish gently in the curry sauce until cooked. Tamarind or lime juice is often added for sourness, and fresh coriander is used for garnish. The dish is usually served with rice.



pav bhaji-( Maharashtra )

From the world-famous pav bhaji to the delicious poha, Maharashtra Food has a lot to offer. Food of Mahrashtra of Maharashtrian cuisine can be broadly classified into two styles- Konkan and Varadi. Konkan being from the coastal region has influences from Goan, Saraswat, Gaud and Malvani regions. Varadi cuisine, on the other hand, belongs to the Vidarbha region. Some spices like Goda Masala, Kokum, Tamarind and Coconut are essential ingredients in the Maharashtrian kitchen. Maharashtrian cuisine is considered to be very diverse as the flavours and spice levels differ in different parts of the state.



Chakhwi-( Tripura )

Chakhwi is a traditional and popular tribal curry dish from Tripura, a state in Northeast India. It is known for its unique preparation involving baking soda (or a traditional alkaline ingredient called Khar) and a mix of local vegetables or meats. The dish is celebrated for being healthy and flavorful, often cooked with bamboo shoot, green papaya, banana stem, jackfruit seeds, and various lentils. Meat such as pork and fish can also be included to enhance the taste. Chakhwi is thickened with sun-dried rice powder called Aayong and generally seasoned with ingredients like ginger, chili, coriander powder, lemon leaves, mustard oil, and clarified butter.



Rugrah-( Jharkhand )

Rugrah (also spelled Rugda or Futka) is a unique and rare mushroom dish indigenous to Jharkhand, a state in Eastern India. It grows naturally during the monsoon season, usually under the soil near Sal trees in the forests, and is highly prized by local tribal communities such as the Santhali and Oraon. Rugrah mushrooms have a rubbery exterior with a soft, yolk-like black inside, which gives them a distinct taste similar to meat, often compared to chicken liver or prawn. Because of this meaty flavor and nutritional richness—high protein, vitamins, and minerals with almost no carbohydrates—Rugrah is sometimes called a "vegetarian meat" or "veg mutton" and serves as an important alternative to non-vegetarian food during periods when locals observe vegetarianism, such as the monsoon. It is a seasonal, highly perishable delicacy that needs to be cooked within hours of harvesting for the best flavor. Preparation often involves making a spicy curry with typical Indian spices and serving it with rice or chapatti.



Chhena Poda-( Odisha )

Chhena Poda is a popular traditional dessert from Odisha, India, made by baking a mixture of fresh cottage cheese (chenna), sugar, and other ingredients until it forms a golden-brown crust. The name "Chhena Poda" literally means "burnt cheese" in Odia, referring to the caramelized top of the cake that forms during baking. The base ingredient is fresh chenna (fresh paneer or cottage cheese) which is crumbled and kneaded with sugar.It often includes flavorings such as cardamom powder and sometimes semolina (sooji) or rice flour for texture.Dry fruits like cashews, almonds, and raisins are commonly added for richness.



Daal Bati Churma-( Rajasthan )

Daal Bati Churma is a traditional and iconic dish from Rajasthan, India, also popular in some adjacent regions including Nepal. The dish is served by dipping bati in hot dal and accompanied by churma on the side. It is hearty, nutritious, and offers a combination of textures and flavors—savory lentils, crunchy wheat balls, and sweet crumbly churma.The preparation usually takes about 1.5 hours from making the dough and baking bati to cooking dal and mixing churma. Dal Bati Churma is especially eaten during festivals and special occasions and is a beloved comfort food in Rajasthan.



Appam-( Kerala )

Appam is a traditional South Indian pancake originating mainly from Kerala and Tamil Nadu, and it is also popular in Sri Lanka where it is known as "hoppers." It is made from a fermented batter of rice and coconut milk, cooked in a special deep, concave pan called an appachatti. The result is a thin, lacy, and slightly crispy edge with a soft, fluffy center, often bowl-shaped.Appams are commonly eaten for breakfast or dinner and are typically served with vegetable or egg curries. The dish has ancient roots, with mentions in Tamil Sangam literature dating back to as early as 300-400 AD. Its name possibly derives from the Sanskrit word apupa, meaning a fried delicacy. Some historical theories link appam to the culinary traditions of Jewish communities in Kerala, while others attribute its origins to ancient Tamil Nadu.



Jadoh-( Meghalaya )

Jadoh is a traditional and popular rice dish from the Khasi community of Meghalaya, Northeast India. The name "Jadoh" combines two Khasi words: "Ja" meaning rice and "Doh" meaning meat, reflecting the dish's core ingredients—rice cooked together with meat, usually pork.Red rice or local varieties of rice are commonly used, giving a distinct texture and flavor.Pork is the traditional meat used, often marinated and cooked with turmeric, black pepper, ginger, garlic, onions, and bay leaves.Sometimes pork blood and pork fat are added to enrich the flavor and give the dish a richer color.Aromatic spices like turmeric, black pepper, and bay leaves are essential to its distinctive taste.



Kangshoi-( Manipur )

Kangshoi is a traditional and popular dish from the northeastern Indian state of Manipur. It is a type of vegetable stew that is usually made with seasonal vegetables, sometimes combined with fish or meat, cooked lightly with minimal spices. The cooking style emphasizes preserving the natural flavors of the ingredients with a simple seasoning of herbs, salt, and sometimes fermented fish (ngari). Made with fresh vegetables like pumpkin, cabbage, drumstick, or colocasia leaves.Often includes herbs such as coriander and sometimes green chiliesLightly cooked with little oil and spices, often using just salt and fermented fish (ngari) for flavor.



Bhutti ka Kees-( Madhya Pradesh )

Bhutti ka Kees is a delicious street-food specialty from Indore, Madhya Pradesh, made from grated fresh corn cooked with spices and milk, resulting in a creamy, mildly spiced snack. The grated corn is sautéed with tempered spices and cooked gently in milk until thickened. The final dish is creamy and comforting with a balance of sweet and spicy notes. It is often enjoyed as a snack or side dish, especially during monsoons.Since you enjoy traditional Indian dishes like papita khar, litti chokha, Manipuri kafuli, and dalbhatichurma, Bhutti ka Kees would be a wonderful flavorful corn-based dish to try from central India.



Bajara Khichri-( Haryana )

Bajara Khichri is a nutritious and wholesome traditional Indian dish made with bajra (pearl millet) and moong dal (green gram lentils), often cooked with mild spices and vegetables. It’s a healthy, fiber-rich meal that’s especially popular in regions where millet is consumed regularly. This dish combines the earthiness of pearl millet with the protein of lentils and is excellent as a filling, comforting meal. It also fits well with your interest in traditional Indian and regional specialties, offering a nutritious vegetarian option from the millet-eating regions.



Thukpa-( Arunachal Pradesh )

Thukpa is a traditional Tibetan noodle soup that is also very popular in regions of Northeast India, Nepal, and Bhutan. It is a warm, comforting soup made with noodles, vegetables, and sometimes meat or chicken, cooked in a flavorful broth. Spiced with turmeric, cumin, chili powder, and garam masala or Sichuan peppercorns depending on the region.Can be vegetarian or include chicken or meat.Garnished with fresh coriander, spring onions, and a squeeze of lemon. Thukpa is a hearty and nutritious meal, perfect for cold or rainy days, combining the soothing warmth of soup with the filling comfort of noodles.



Bisi Bele Bath-( Karnataka )

Bisi Bele Bath is a traditional and iconic dish from Karnataka, India, renowned for its rich flavors and wholesome ingredients. Literally meaning "hot lentil rice," this dish blends cooked rice, toor dal (split pigeon peas), mixed vegetables, tamarind pulp, jaggery, and a distinctive spice mix called bisi bele bath powder. The dish is often served hot, garnished with ghee, roasted cashews, and fresh coriander.The preparation involves cooking rice and dal until soft and mushy, preferably in a pressure cooker. Meanwhile, vegetables such as carrots, peas, beans, potatoes, tomatoes, and onions are sautéed and combined with tamarind water and jaggery to provide a perfect balance of tangy and sweet notes. The bisi bele bath masala powder—a blend of roasted spices like coriander seeds, red chilies, cumin, fenugreek, cinnamon, cloves, dry coconut, and poppy seeds—lends a deep, aromatic flavor.



Pork with bamboo shoots-( Nagaland )

Pork with bamboo shoots is a traditional and beloved dish from Northeast India, particularly among the Naga tribes, celebrated for its hearty, smoky flavors and distinctive use of bamboo shoots. This dish masterfully combines tender pork, often from fatty cuts like pork belly or smoked pork, with either fresh or dried bamboo shoots, which provide a crunchy texture and a mildly tangy, earthy flavor.To prepare this dish, dried bamboo shoots are first soaked in water to rehydrate and soften them. Pork is cut into bite-sized pieces and cooked slowly, often in a deep pot, over low heat until the fat starts to melt, creating a rich base for the curry. The soaked bamboo shoots, along with coarsely ground garlic and soaked dried red chillies (sometimes Naga King chillies for extra heat), are added to the pot. Salt and water are adjusted based on the amount of fat and moisture in the pork. The mixture is then simmered gently for about 15 to 20 minutes, allowing the flavors of pork fat, spicy chilies, aromatic garlic, and tangy bamboo to meld perfectly.



Misa Mach Poora-( Mizoram )

Misa Mach Poora is a traditional and beloved dish from Mizoram, a state in Northeast India, known for its simple yet flavorful preparation of grilled or roasted shrimps. The name "Misa Mach Poora" translates roughly to "grilled shrimp" in the Mizo language, reflecting the dish’s primary ingredient and cooking method. This dish is a staple in Mizo cuisine and is cherished for its smoky aroma and succulent taste, often enjoyed with steamed rice.The preparation of Misa Mach Poora is straightforward, involving only a few key ingredients that highlight the freshness of the shrimp. The shrimps are peeled and deveined before being marinated in a mix of mustard oil, turmeric powder, red chili powder, garlic, ginger, lemon juice, and salt. This marinade infuses the seafood with subtle heat, tanginess, and aromatic flavors typical of Northeast Indian cooking.



Aamat-( Chhattisgarh )

Aamat is a traditional and popular dish from the Bastar region of Chhattisgarh, India. Often referred to as the "Sambar of Bastar," Aamat is a hearty and flavorful mixed vegetable curry made with a variety of local vegetables, including bamboo shoots, which give it a distinctive taste and aroma.The dish is characterized by its use of fresh, seasonal vegetables cooked together with a rich ginger-garlic paste and an assortment of spices. Traditionally, Aamat is cooked inside bamboo containers or with bamboo shoots, which infuses the curry with a unique earthy flavor that reflects the forested landscape of Bastar.



Tunday Kebab-( Uttar Pradesh )

Tunday Kebab, also known as Galouti Kebab, is a legendary dish from Lucknow, Uttar Pradesh, deeply rooted in Awadhi cuisine. Its history dates back to the late 19th and early 20th centuries during the reign of Nawab Wajid Ali Shah. The story behind Tunday Kebab is as fascinating as its melt-in-mouth texture and rich flavors.The name "Tunday" comes from Haji Murad Ali, a one-armed chef ('tunday' meaning one-armed in the local slang), who cooked in the royal kitchens of Awadh. The Nawab, aged and having lost his teeth, requested a kebab so tender that he could eat it without chewing. In response, Murad Ali crafted the Galouti Kebab, a finely minced meat delicately spiced with a secret blend of over 100 Indian and exotic spices, including traditional aphrodisiacs. The meat was so soft and flavorful that it melted in the mouth, earning the kebab its alternate name, "Galouti," meaning "soft to the point of melting."



Momos-( Sikkim )

Momos are a beloved traditional dish from Sikkim and the Northeastern Himalayan region of India. They are steamed dumplings made from a thin wheat flour dough filled with a variety of ingredients such as minced meat, vegetables, or cheese, reflecting the influence of Tibetan and Nepali cuisines in the region.To prepare momos, the dough is made by mixing all-purpose flour, water, and a pinch of salt, kneaded into a smooth and firm consistency. The filling typically includes finely chopped vegetables like cabbage, carrots, and spring onions, combined with garlic, ginger, soy sauce, and black pepper for flavor. For non-vegetarian versions, minced chicken or pork is commonly used, mixed similarly with aromatic spices and herbs.
