Unity in Diversity

India’s hallmark is its unity in diversity: across 28 states and 8 union territories, people practice different religions, celebrate vibrant festivals, and speak over 1,600 languages and dialects. Despite these differences, a shared respect for each culture and tradition weaves Indians together, fostering harmony and resilience

Religions in India

Temple

Hinduism is the largest and most practiced religion in India, with about 80% of the country's population identifying as Hindu. It is one of the world's oldest religions, originating over 3,000 years ago in the Indian subcontinent. Hinduism is characterized by beliefs in concepts such as dharma (moral duties), karma (actions and consequences), reincarnation, and moksha (liberation from the cycle of rebirth).

Church

Christianity is the third-largest religion in India, with about 2.3% of the population adhering to it, which amounts to roughly 28 million people as of recent estimates. Christianity has a long history in India, dating back to ancient times, with early communities especially in the southern states like Kerala and Goa, as well as in the northeastern states such as Nagaland, Mizoram, and Meghalaya where Christians form majorities.

Masjid

Muslim religion in India, primarily Islam, arrived in the region around the 7th century CE through Arab traders and the conquest of Sindh in 712 CE. Islam is now the second-largest religion in India, comprising about 14% of the population. Indian Muslims follow diverse traditions, largely Sunni and Shia. Historically, Muslim rule expanded with the Delhi Sultanate and the Mughal Empire, influencing Indian culture, architecture, and society.




Buddha Temple

Buddhism originated in India around the 5th to 6th century BCE, founded by Siddhartha Gautama, known as the Buddha or "The Enlightened One," who was born near present-day Nepal. It arose in a period of social and religious change when many people were dissatisfied with the traditional Vedic rituals and caste system.

Gurdwara

Sikhism is a monotheistic religion founded in the late 15th century in the Punjab region of India by Guru Nanak (1469–1539). It emerged as a distinct faith emphasizing devotion to one God, equality, social justice, and rejection of caste distinctions and ritualism. Sikhism teaches the importance of honest living, meditation on God's name, and community service

Jain Mandir

Jainism is an ancient Indian religion that originated around the 7th to 5th century BCE in the Ganges basin. It was propagated by 24 Tirthankaras, spiritual teachers who attained liberation, with Mahavira (c. 599–527 BCE) being the last and most prominent. Jainism emphasizes non-violence (ahimsa), asceticism, and truth, teaching liberation through strict ethical conduct, meditation, and self-discipline.

Traditions in India

India is renowned for its vibrant religious traditions, with cultural practices woven deeply into daily life across its diverse communities. Major religious traditions include Hinduism, Islam, Christianity, Sikhism, Buddhism, and Jainism, each contributing unique rituals, festivals, and social customs.Some common Hindu traditions involve daily puja (worship), recitation of scriptures, yoga, meditation, charitable acts, and festival celebrations like Diwali and Holi. Muslim traditions include daily prayers (salat), observing Ramadan, and celebrations such as Eid. Christian customs center on church attendance, major feasts like Christmas and Easter, and various community services. Sikh practices focus on equality, community service, meditation, and observance of the Five Ks, while Jain and Buddhist traditions emphasize non-violence, meditation, and ethical living
